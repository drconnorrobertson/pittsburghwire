Gecko Robotics Opens Sewickley Manufacturing Hub, Bringing Defense Tech to Pittsburgh's Doorstep

The Pittsburgh-born unicorn is expanding beyond inspection robotics into a new 10,000-square-foot Sewickley facility where AI, robots, and skilled workers will help American defense manufacturers build critical systems faster.

By Marcus Delacroix July 21, 2026 4 min read

Pittsburgh's skyline at night — the city's tech sector continues to expand with Gecko Robotics anchoring new manufacturing growth in Sewickley. // Photo: Dllu / CC BY-SA 4.0 / Wikimedia Commons

Gecko Robotics, the Pittsburgh-born AI and robotics company that has spent a decade making infrastructure safer one climbing robot at a time, is planting its next flag just twelve miles up the Ohio River. The company announced last week that it will open a 10,000-square-foot manufacturing and integration facility in Sewickley — a move that cements Western Pennsylvania's place at the center of America's defense robotics renaissance.

The new facility, which Gecko spokesperson Steve Bourner confirms is already operational with robots running inside, will serve as a production and integration hub for the company's growing defense manufacturing business. It is where Gecko's proprietary hardware and its Cantilever AI software platform will come together to help defense contractors build components more efficiently — and, by extension, more quickly than traditional manual methods allow.

"The future will be built by combining advanced robotics, AI, and skilled workers into a single manufacturing ecosystem. By bringing together world-class partners, software, and automation in Pittsburgh, we're helping manufacturers produce the critical systems our military depends on faster, more safely, and with higher quality."

Jake Loosararian, CEO & Co-Founder, Gecko Robotics

CEO Jake Loosararian announced the Sewickley hub last week at the Pennsylvania Defense and Innovation Summit in Carlisle, where hundreds of millions in new investment commitments were unveiled across the state. For Loosararian — who built Gecko from a college dorm-room idea into a company now valued at over $1.25 billion — the announcement carried a deeper message: Pittsburgh tech companies have an obligation to grow at home.

From Inspection Robots to Defense Manufacturing

Gecko made its name sending wall-climbing robots into the places humans cannot safely go — submarine hulls, aircraft carrier decks, power plant boilers — gathering inspection data that would otherwise require costly shutdowns and risky manual work. That core business has expanded steadily into U.S. Navy surface fleet maintenance, nuclear missile silo modernization, and energy infrastructure monitoring for some of the country's largest utilities.

The Sewickley facility represents a meaningful pivot into manufacturing itself. Rather than only inspecting what others build, Gecko now aims to accelerate how defense components are made, using the same Cantilever platform — its AI system that turns raw sensor data into actionable operational insight — to optimize production lines the same way it optimizes asset health monitoring. It is, in the company's own framing, the logical next step: if you understand how critical systems degrade, you are well-positioned to build them better from the start.

Staying Pittsburgh, On Principle

Loosararian has been unusually vocal — for a tech CEO — about the importance of keeping high-growth companies rooted in Pittsburgh rather than letting them drift toward San Francisco or New York as they scale. The Sewickley announcement is the most concrete expression yet of that philosophy. Sewickley is not a satellite office in another market; it is a production facility within driving distance of Gecko's Pittsburgh headquarters, adding industrial capacity to a region that is learning, facility by facility, to think of itself as a manufacturing hub again — this time for the technologies that define the twenty-first century rather than the twentieth.

The timing is no accident. Pittsburgh's defense tech ecosystem has deepened considerably over the past two years, buoyed by federal investment in AI and autonomous systems, a concentrated university research base at Carnegie Mellon and Pitt, and a pool of engineering talent that rivals any city in the country. Gecko's Sewickley hub adds a physical production anchor to that ecosystem, giving younger defense-adjacent startups a model to point to: it is possible to build serious manufacturing capacity here, without leaving.

For a borough like Sewickley, known historically for its leafy streetscapes and proximity to Pittsburgh's business elite, the arrival of a robotics manufacturing operation signals something new. It suggests that the economic geography of the Pittsburgh region — long defined by what lies within the city limits — is expanding outward, with technology companies choosing communities along the Ohio River corridor for reasons that go beyond square footage and tax incentives.

Gecko says the Sewickley facility will be fully open within weeks. The robots, as Bourner noted, are already at work.
