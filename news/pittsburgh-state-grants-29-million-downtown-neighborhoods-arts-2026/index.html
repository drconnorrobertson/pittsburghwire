Pennsylvania Awards $29.3 Million to Transform Pittsburgh's Downtown, Parks, and Neighborhoods

Fourteen projects spanning the Hill District, North Shore, downtown, and beyond will receive state funding for housing, arts, parks, and institutional upgrades — the largest single tranche of Redevelopment Assistance Capital Program grants directed at the city in recent memory.

By Daniel Park July 20, 2026 5 min read

Downtown Pittsburgh, the focal point of several new state-funded revitalization projects. // Photo: Dllu / CC BY-SA 4.0 / Wikimedia Commons

Pittsburgh is about to get a lot more to look at, live in, and linger over. Last Thursday, State Rep. Aerion Abney announced that more than $29.35 million in state grants — disbursed through Pennsylvania's Redevelopment Assistance Capital Program — will fund 14 distinct projects across his Pittsburgh district, ranging from a new civic arts lawn in the Cultural District to six affordable homes in the Hill District to a veterinary hospital at the National Aviary on the North Side.

The sheer breadth of the package is notable. In a single announcement, Abney touched virtually every major neighborhood thread the city has been pulling on: downtown revitalization, arts infrastructure, office-to-residential conversion, riverfront accessibility, and institutional growth at Duquesne University and Pressley Ridge. It's a comprehensive bet that Pittsburgh's momentum is real — and that the state is ready to put real money behind it.

"These investments are about creating places where families can gather, businesses can grow, students can learn, and neighborhoods can thrive."

State Rep. Aerion Abney, D-Allegheny

Arts Landing Takes the Largest Grant

The single biggest award in the package — $8 million — goes to the Pittsburgh Cultural Trust for Arts Landing, a planned public gathering space in the heart of the Cultural District. The project will create a performance lawn, recreational amenities, and integrated public art installations designed to activate the Cultural District year-round, not just on show nights. When complete, Arts Landing is expected to become one of downtown's primary outdoor civic spaces, complementing the Trust's existing constellation of theaters and galleries along Penn and Liberty avenues.

Running a close second is a $5 million grant to the Pittsburgh Downtown Partnership for a comprehensive overhaul of Market Square. One of the city's oldest public spaces, Market Square will see improved lighting, modernized infrastructure, flexible event configurations, and enhanced accessibility. The Partnership has long managed the Square's programming calendar — from farmers markets to holiday festivals — and the capital investment will let the physical space finally catch up with the ambition of those events.

Gulf Tower and Grant Street: Downtown Housing Gets a Push

Two separate grants totaling $7.5 million will accelerate the conversion of the Gulf Tower, the distinctive stepped skyscraper at 707 Grant Street, into a mixed-use development. A $4 million award funds housing, retail, hospitality, and infrastructure work inside the building itself, while an accompanying $3.5 million grant focuses on retail and dining at street level and streetscape improvements to the surrounding pedestrian environment. Together, the two grants represent one of the most significant investments yet in downtown Pittsburgh's ongoing office-to-residential push.

A separate $3 million award will convert underused office space on Grant Street into housing — including affordable units — with street-level retail. Taken together, the downtown housing grants represent a recognition that density and vibrancy reinforce each other, and that getting more people living in Pittsburgh's core is the surest path to a downtown that hums on a Tuesday evening.

The $29.3 Million Package: Key Numbers
14 Distinct projects funded across Pittsburgh neighborhoods and institutions
$8M To the Pittsburgh Cultural Trust for Arts Landing, the largest single award
$7.5M Combined grants to advance the Gulf Tower mixed-use conversion
$5M For a comprehensive Market Square modernization led by the Pittsburgh Downtown Partnership
6 New affordable homes to be built by the Charles Street Area Corporation in the Hill District

Neighborhoods, Institutions, and the Riverfront

Beyond downtown, the package fans out across the city in ways that reflect Pittsburgh's broader priorities. In the Hill District, the city will receive $500,000 to build the Bedford Choice Linear Park — a new green corridor in a neighborhood that has historically lacked equitable access to park space. The Charles Street Area Corporation, also in the Hill District, was awarded $500,000 to construct six affordable homes, adding to the neighborhood's ongoing revitalization without displacing the people who built it.

On the North Shore, Riverlife will use $500,000 to improve accessibility, restore riverfront habitat, and add play features at Allegheny Landing — a beloved stretch of river access that serves as a gateway to the city's broader trail network. The investment keeps Allegheny Landing competitive with the riverfront amenities emerging on the South Side and in Hazelwood Green.

Institutional grants round out the package. Duquesne University's planned health sciences building received $1 million, as did Pressley Ridge — the Pittsburgh organization that serves students with behavioral and mental health needs — for campus improvements. The Pittsburgh Symphony's Heinz Hall will receive $550,000 for infrastructure upgrades, the Mattress Factory museum $300,000 for accessibility and gallery improvements, and the National Aviary $500,000 for a new veterinary hospital that will serve the birds under its care and support its conservation mission.

A Citywide Vote of Confidence

What's striking about this grant announcement isn't any single project — it's the density of investment across so many corners of the city at once. Pittsburgh has spent the better part of a decade rebuilding the case that it is a serious place to bet on: in technology, in healthcare, in higher education, and increasingly in the quality of everyday urban life. This package, drawn from a program designed to catalyze exactly this kind of broad-based civic reinvestment, is the state's clearest signal yet that the bet is paying off.

"I'm proud to have advocated for investments that will benefit residents today while positioning Pittsburgh for continued success in the years ahead," Abney said. For the neighborhoods and institutions that will receive these funds, the work is just beginning — but the resources are now in place.
