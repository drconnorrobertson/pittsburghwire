UPMC Opens $85M Heart Institute at Children's Hospital, Bringing World-Class Pediatric Cardiac Care to Lawrenceville

The first addition to the Children's Hospital campus since its 2009 move to Penn Avenue features three catheterization labs, the region's first pediatric cardiac rehab area, and a pioneering side-by-side MRI and cath lab setup that no other regional hospital can match.

By Margaret Donnelly June 5, 2026 4 min read

UPMC Children's Hospital of Pittsburgh, Penn Avenue, Lawrenceville. // Photo: Wikimedia Commons contributor / CC BY-SA 2.5 / Wikimedia Commons

On a bright June morning in Lawrenceville, UPMC CEO Leslie Davis stood before a crowd of physicians, families, and civic leaders and declared the opening of something Pittsburgh has been building toward for years. "Today, we open the doors not just to a new facility but to a new era of heart care at UPMC Children's," Davis told the gathering. The $85 million Heart Institute at UPMC Children's Hospital of Pittsburgh officially opened Thursday, adding a landmark chapter to the institution's long run as the region's premier destination for pediatric medicine.

The new facility — the first major expansion of the Children's Hospital campus since the hospital relocated from Oakland to Penn Avenue in 2009 — is designed not merely as a renovation but as a statement of intent. Pittsburgh, long known as a global leader in transplant medicine and surgical innovation, is now staking a claim to become one of the world's leading centers for pediatric cardiac care. The Heart Institute's three state-of-the-art cardiac catheterization labs, combined with the region's first dedicated pediatric cardiac rehabilitation area, represent an investment that hospital officials say will serve families from across the country and around the world.

The centerpiece of the new institute is an engineering feat that no other hospital in western Pennsylvania can currently offer: an MRI suite and a cardiac catheterization lab positioned directly adjacent to one another. Previously, pediatric heart patients requiring both an MRI and a catheterization had to undergo two separate hospital admissions. Under the new setup, both procedures can be performed under a single round of anesthesia.

"Now we can do both at the same time. It's going to simplify the care and make it more efficient."

Dr. Victor Morell, Chief of UPMC Pediatric Cardiothoracic Surgery

For Dr. Victor Morell, chief of the UPMC pediatric cardiothoracic surgery division, the reduction in anesthesia exposure is more than a logistical improvement. Children with congenital heart conditions face elevated risk from each round of general anesthesia, and even a small reduction in the number of procedures requiring sedation can meaningfully improve patient safety. "Now we can do both at the same time," he said. "I feel like I'm in Disney right now."

Among the families celebrating Thursday was the Callan family of Brentwood. Ethan and Kelsey Callan have navigated three heart surgeries for their three-year-old daughter, Artemis, who was born with a defective heart valve. "It's a scary time in your life," Ethan said. "But everybody here has your best interests at heart. We could not be happier with the care that we've gotten here."

What This Means for Pittsburgh

The Heart Institute is designed to serve not just Allegheny County but the full arc of families who travel to Pittsburgh for specialized pediatric cardiac care. Approximately one-third of all patients seen by UPMC Children's cardiologists each year arrive from outside the metro region. The new facility includes a dedicated conferencing suite built to support real-time collaboration between Pittsburgh physicians and cardiac specialists around the world.

A second construction phase remains underway and is expected to complete in 2027, adding a cardiac rehabilitation gym, outpatient clinic, and eight echocardiography labs to the Penn Avenue campus. For a city that has spent decades building its reputation on the strength of its medical and research institutions, Thursday's ribbon-cutting on Penn Avenue is one more piece of evidence that Pittsburgh's best days in medicine are still ahead.
