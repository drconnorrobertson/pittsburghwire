Pittsburgh's Paper: How the Tribune-Review Stepped Up to Become the City's Newspaper of Record

When the Pittsburgh Post-Gazette published its final edition in May after nearly 240 years, the Tribune-Review made a decision: return to print, add a dozen reporters, and become the local news institution the city needs.

By Amara Osei-Bonsu July 1, 2026 4 min read

Downtown Pittsburgh, the city the Tribune-Review is now committed to covering as its primary newspaper of record. // Photo: Dllu / CC BY-SA 4.0 / Wikimedia Commons

On the morning of May 9, 2026, Pittsburghers who walked into their corner stores found something they had not seen in a decade: a weekly Tribune-Review, printed in Pittsburgh, about Pittsburgh, waiting on the newsstand. Six days earlier, the Pittsburgh Post-Gazette had published its last edition. The city that had supported two major daily newspapers for generations was suddenly down to one voice in print, and that voice had made a choice to show up.

Trib Total Media's decision to launch a Saturday Pittsburgh print edition was not made in a vacuum. The company had been watching the Post-Gazette's decline, its three-year newsroom strike, and Block Communications' eventual decision to cease operations after sustaining more than $350 million in losses over two decades. When it became clear the Post-Gazette would not survive, the Tribune-Review moved quickly. The new weekly Pittsburgh edition was announced in January, planned through the winter, and in the hands of readers the very week after the Post-Gazette closed.

"Pittsburgh is a city that deserves a newspaper of record. We intend to be that paper."

Trib Total Media, on the launch of the Pittsburgh print edition

The edition's content reflects a deliberate commitment to depth. Every Saturday, readers get original, local reporting focused on Pittsburgh and Allegheny County: city government, education, transportation, public safety, business, and health care. An expanded health section acknowledges Pittsburgh's status as a national leader in medical research and hospital innovation. The sports section features the Trib's roster of columnists alongside comprehensive coverage of the Steelers, Pirates, and Penguins. And the comics page includes the work of Pittsburgh's own Joe Wos, the cartoonist and storyteller who has been one of the city's most beloved creative voices for years.

The print edition is not Trib Total Media's only investment in Pittsburgh journalism. The company added approximately a dozen new journalists to its newsroom to support the expanded coverage. These are beat reporters and editors with specific assignments across the city's neighborhoods and institutions. For a news organization that had eliminated its print edition in Pittsburgh back in 2016 and shifted entirely to digital, this represents a fundamental reorientation toward the city.

A Pivot Built on Conviction

What makes the Tribune-Review's move significant is not just the logistics of printing and distribution. It is the editorial conviction behind it. Local journalism in American cities has been hollowing out for twenty years. Pittsburgh, with its deep civic identity and its culture of neighborhood investment, is not the kind of city that should be left with a news void. The Trib's leadership appears to understand that. The new edition is available at retail locations and newsstands across the region every Saturday, and a home delivery subscription and e-edition option are both available for readers who want the paper delivered directly.

The coverage areas the Tribune-Review has prioritized are not accidental. Business, health care, transportation, and education are the beats that matter most to a working city navigating significant change. Pittsburgh is in the middle of a decade-long economic transformation, from legacy steel and manufacturing toward robotics, AI, life sciences, and a booming hospitality sector. Reporting on that transformation requires journalists who know the city's neighborhoods, its institutions, and its people, not just wire feeds and aggregated content.

For Pittsburghers who have spent years watching the local media landscape shrink, the Tribune-Review's decision to go bigger, rather than smaller, in the wake of the Post-Gazette's closure is a meaningful signal. The city's story will be told. There will be reporters at City Hall and in the Hill District and at the airport and at the research labs in Oakland. There will be a Saturday paper. For a city as proud of its identity as Pittsburgh, that is not a small thing.
