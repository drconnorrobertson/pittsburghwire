CMU and Mark Cuban Bring $1.75 Million AI Pitch Competition to Pittsburgh for NFL Draft Week

Carnegie Mellon University, the AI Strike Team, and the Pittsburgh Steelers are teaming up to host a high-stakes startup showcase at the Robotics Innovation Center in Hazelwood Green, putting the city's artificial intelligence credentials on a national stage just as hundreds of thousands of football fans arrive.

By Connor Robertson April 18, 2026 4 min read

Pittsburgh's AI and robotics ecosystem takes center stage during NFL Draft Week // Photo: Unsplash

Pittsburgh has long been one of the country's most important cities for artificial intelligence research. Next week, it plans to prove that to the rest of the world. On April 22, one day before the NFL Draft kicks off at Point State Park, Carnegie Mellon University and the AI Strike Team will host "Powering the Future of Sport: A Draft Week Showcase" at CMU's Robotics Innovation Center in Hazelwood Green. The invite-only event will bring together global investors, AI founders, Fortune 500 executives, civic leaders, and sports industry heavyweights for a day of live demonstrations, panel discussions, and a startup pitch competition with a $1.75 million prize pool.

The centerpiece of the showcase is the Forge to Field AI Pitch Competition, in which emerging AI companies from across the country will present their sports-adjacent innovations to a panel of judges headlined by entrepreneur and Mt. Lebanon native Mark Cuban and Dick's Sporting Goods Executive Chairman Ed Stack. The startups are working at the intersection of artificial intelligence, robotics, and the sports industry, covering areas like athletic performance, player safety, training technology, logistics, media production, and fan engagement. Winners will receive a share of the prize pool, which includes up to $1 million in Amazon Web Services cloud credits, with the condition that winning companies relocate their operations to Pennsylvania.

The event's speaker lineup reads like a who's who of Pittsburgh leadership. The program opens with a session called "Pittsburgh's Moment," featuring remarks from CMU President Farnam Jahanian, Pennsylvania Governor Josh Shapiro, Allegheny County Executive Sara Innamorato, and Pittsburgh Mayor Corey O'Connor. Steelers legend Jerome Bettis will anchor a panel discussion on the intersection of technology and professional sports. U.S. Senator Dave McCormick will close out the day with remarks on the federal government's role in supporting AI innovation.

"Pittsburgh has always been a city that builds things. Now we are building the future of artificial intelligence."

Carnegie Mellon University

The choice of Hazelwood Green as the venue is itself a statement about where Pittsburgh's innovation economy is heading. The 178-acre former steel mill site along the Monongahela River has been reimagined as a mixed-use technology and research district, and CMU's Robotics Innovation Center sits at its heart. Hosting a nationally visible AI event there signals that Pittsburgh's tech corridor now stretches well beyond Oakland and the Strip District into neighborhoods that were, not long ago, defined by the industry that preceded this one.

For the AI Strike Team, a coalition working to position Pittsburgh as a global hub for real-world AI, the Draft Week timing is deliberate. An estimated 200,000 to 300,000 visitors will be in downtown Pittsburgh for the three-day Draft beginning April 23, and the national media coverage that comes with it provides an audience that no standalone tech conference could match. The strategy is straightforward: while the football world is watching Pittsburgh, make sure they also see the city's AI and robotics credentials.

The numbers support the ambition. The Pittsburgh region raised $2.29 billion in startup funding in 2025, including a record $2.06 billion in institutional venture capital. The city ranks seventh nationally in AI and autonomous vehicle funding per capita, a position built on decades of foundational research at Carnegie Mellon and the University of Pittsburgh, and reinforced by the presence of companies like Aurora Innovation, Duolingo, and Abridge, all of which have chosen to keep their headquarters here.

What makes next week's showcase different from a typical industry event is its insistence on tying AI to something tangible. The startups pitching are not presenting theoretical research; they are demonstrating technologies that operate in the physical world, at scale, under the pressures of live competition and real-time decision making. That focus on applied, real-world AI is precisely the niche Pittsburgh has carved for itself over the past decade, and the Draft Week Showcase is the city's most visible effort yet to claim it on a national stage. For a city that has spent years doing the work, the timing could not be better.
