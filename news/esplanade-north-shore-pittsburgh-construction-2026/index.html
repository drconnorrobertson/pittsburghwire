Pittsburgh's $740 Million Esplanade Rises on the North Shore, Bringing a Ferris Wheel and 4,500 Jobs to the Chateau Waterfront

Construction is now underway on Piatt Companies' transformative 15-acre riverfront development in Chateau, Pittsburgh's most ambitious mixed-use project in a generation — and the city's skyline will never look quite the same.

By James Calloway July 20, 2026 4 min read

Pittsburgh's North Shore, where the Esplanade will transform 15 post-industrial acres into a riverfront destination. // Photo: Dough4872 / CC BY-SA 4.0 / Wikimedia Commons

The earth movers arrived in Chateau before the first snow fell. Now, with summer well underway, the transformation of Pittsburgh's North Shore is no longer a rendering on a planning commission wall — it is a construction site, a rising thing, a promise kept after nearly a decade of work by Lucas Piatt and his team at Piatt Companies. The $740 million Esplanade project is officially under construction, and the city it will help shape is already coming into view.

Piatt Companies broke ground on the Esplanade in December 2025 on a 15-acre stretch of post-industrial land in the Chateau neighborhood, tucked along the Allegheny River not far from Acrisure Stadium. The project has been nine years in the making, threading through city planning commissions, financing rounds, community input sessions, and the kind of sustained negotiation that defines major urban development. What has emerged is among the most ambitious mixed-use projects Pittsburgh has seen in a generation.

"Advancing a project of this magnitude requires coordination across government, private industry, labor and community stakeholders — each contributing to a shared vision for growth, opportunity, and long-term value."

Lucas Piatt, CEO, Piatt Companies

The first phase of construction — now actively underway — will deliver what is already the project's signature feature: a 200-foot-tall Ferris wheel with climate-controlled cabs, positioned along the riverfront as a year-round attraction. Alongside it, the phase will produce up to 750 rental apartments, including units reserved for families earning 80 percent of the area's median income, as well as 160,000 square feet of retail space and The Current, a riverfront dining and culinary hub with an adjacent amphitheater. A 120,000-square-foot innovation workspace and a parking garage round out the first phase, which is anticipated to open in 2028.

A Second Life for Post-Industrial Ground

The Chateau neighborhood has long occupied a curious position in Pittsburgh's geography — close to everything, activated by little. The site the Esplanade now occupies spent decades as industrial land, gradually falling idle as Pittsburgh's manufacturing economy receded. What Piatt Companies is building in its place is designed to reconnect this stretch of the North Shore to the river and to the broader city.

Phase two, anticipated for completion in 2029, will add 126 riverfront condominiums, a 225-unit hotel, a marina with houseboats and day slips, a marine safety facility, and a public pier extending into the Allegheny. A second tranche of apartments and a specialty entertainment destination are also planned for the later phase. Taken together, the two phases will remake more than a mile of North Shore waterfront into a year-round urban district.

What It Means for Pittsburgh

An economic analysis conducted by The Economy League of Greater Pittsburgh projects that the Esplanade will support 9,300 jobs during its construction phases and generate 4,500 permanent positions once fully operational. The development is expected to contribute $547.7 million to the regional economy and could catalyze an additional $1.5 billion in local spending over time. Those are not the numbers of a single building — they are the numbers of a neighborhood.

For Pittsburgh, the Esplanade represents something more than square footage and job counts. It represents the continued closing of a gap between what this city has long been and what it is increasingly becoming. Pittsburgh's riverfront has always been its most underutilized asset. The three rivers that made this place industrial also made it beautiful, and for decades the waterfronts sat largely fallow, accessible in patches but never cohesive, never activated in the way that a city of Pittsburgh's density and ambition deserves.

The Esplanade changes that. When the Ferris wheel begins its rotations in 2028 and The Current opens its doors to the Allegheny, a stretch of Pittsburgh's North Shore that once sat quiet will become one of the most visited destinations in the region. For Chateau, for the North Shore, and for the city at large, that is not a small thing. It is the kind of transformation that compounds — drawing residents, visitors, businesses, and investment in waves that no single project fully predicts but every successful one generates. Pittsburgh has been building toward this. Now the building has begun.
