Heinz History Center Breaks Ground on $80 Million Expansion

The Smithsonian-affiliated museum's 92,000-square-foot addition will bring a new Museum of African American History, an interactive Fred Rogers exhibit, and a transformed Penn Avenue plaza — positioning Pittsburgh's most-visited history institution as one of the largest in the nation by 2028.

By The Pittsburgh Wire Staff July 2, 2026 4 min read
HISTORY

Rendering of the Heinz History Center's new Penn Avenue Plaza, featuring large-scale industrial and military artifacts. Rendering courtesy of Desmone Architects.

On a warm Thursday morning at the corner of Penn Avenue and 13th Street in the Strip District, Pittsburgh's leaders gathered with shovels in hand to mark the beginning of something large — in both ambition and square footage. The Senator John Heinz History Center officially broke ground on an $80 million, 92,000-square-foot expansion that will transform the Smithsonian-affiliated museum into one of the largest history institutions in the country.

The project, part of the museum's "History for All" capital campaign, is slated for completion in 2028. When finished, it will nearly double the museum's footprint and add a sweeping collection of new spaces: a redesigned Great Hall with café and 150-seat theater on the ground floor, an 8,500-square-foot outdoor rooftop terrace for events, smart classrooms designed to handle a 50 percent increase in school field trips, and a new outdoor Penn Avenue Plaza anchored by a Civil War-era Rodman cannon forged in Pittsburgh and a massive steel ladle drawn from the city's industrial past.

By the Numbers
$80M Total cost of the History for All capital campaign expansion
92,000 Square feet of new space added to the Smithsonian-affiliated museum
70% Of the $80 million goal already raised, anchored by 100% board participation
50% Projected increase in school field trips enabled by new smart classrooms
2028 Target completion year for the full expansion project

Among the expansion's signature additions is a fourth-floor Museum of African American History, a dedicated institution within the institution that will explore the Black experience and its essential role in shaping Pittsburgh and the nation. Museum officials describe it as drawing on the History Center's expansive African American collection to become "one of the most significant repositories of Black history in the nation." Alongside that flagship space, the expansion includes an interactive Fred Rogers exhibition featuring original sets and artifacts from Mister Rogers' Neighborhood, updated galleries for the Franco Harris Sports Museum, and expanded programming from the museum's Italian American Program and Rauh Jewish Archives.

"Penn Avenue will be a cultural corridor from the Strip District all the way to Point State Park, with a stop off at Arts Landing."

Andy Masich, President & CEO, Senator John Heinz History Center

Andy Masich, the History Center's president and CEO, framed the expansion as more than a building project. "Penn Avenue will be a cultural corridor from the Strip District all the way to Point State Park, with a stop off at Arts Landing," he said at the groundbreaking. "Penn Avenue will be activated like never before, and the History Center is the connector, the hyphen, the link, between the Strip District and the Cultural District." That vision aligns with the recent opening of Arts Landing, the new public arts and performance space in the Cultural District, and positions the museum as an anchor institution along one of the city's most important pedestrian corridors.

A Moment Built on Momentum

The groundbreaking comes at a high-water mark for the museum. USA Today named the Heinz History Center the No. 1 history museum in America for the third consecutive year, and the institution recently earned national accreditation from the American Alliance of Museums. Allegheny County Executive Sara Innamorato, who attended Thursday's ceremony, noted the timing is charged with national significance as well, with the country approaching its 250th birthday this July. "I think it's so important in this moment that we have an extra appreciation for America's story, and who's telling it, and how it's being told," she said. "This new exhibition and event space will bring history to life in more dynamic and engaging ways, and expand our capacity to be able to elevate voices and stories that have too often gone untold."

Campaign co-chair Bill Lambert pointed to the breadth of the museum's ambition. "The $80 million campaign will dramatically expand our reach, creating a more welcoming, relevant, accessible, inspirational, entrepreneurial and sustainable museum for our community here, and for the visitors that come to Pittsburgh from all over the world," he said. "Our expansion is a promise to offer in perpetuity broad access to educational, inspirational programs, including stories from diverse communities."

Fundraising for the campaign is already well advanced, with 70 percent of the $80 million goal secured. Every member of the museum's board of trustees has made a contribution, and major donors include the Heinz Endowments and Daniel and Carole Kamin, the same philanthropic family whose $65 million gift recently renamed the Carnegie Science Center to the Kamin Science Center. The remaining 30 percent — roughly $24 million — is now being sought from the broader Pittsburgh community. For a city that has long punched above its weight in cultural institutions, this expansion feels less like a stretch and more like an affirmation of what Pittsburgh has always known about itself: that its story is worth telling, and worth telling well.
