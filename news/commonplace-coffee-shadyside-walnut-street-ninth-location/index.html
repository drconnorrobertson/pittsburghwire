Commonplace Coffee Brings Its Ninth Cafe and a New Bakery Hub to Shadyside's Walnut Street

The beloved Pittsburgh roaster takes over the space from Georgie's Corner Cafe, relocates a commercial bakery from Indiana, PA, and deepens its roots in one of the city's most walkable neighborhoods.

By Marcus Webb July 2, 2026 4 min read

The 5700 block of Walnut Street in Shadyside, steps from where Commonplace Coffee's ninth location will open this summer at 5743 Walnut St. // Photo: Cbaile19 / CC0 / Wikimedia Commons

Commonplace Coffee is coming to Shadyside. The Pittsburgh roaster, which has spent more than a decade building one of the region's most admired specialty coffee networks, announced this summer that it will open its ninth retail location at 5743 Walnut Street, in the space that until recently housed Georgie's Corner Cafe. The move is more than an address change. Commonplace plans to relocate a significant portion of its commercial bakery operations from Indiana, Pennsylvania into the Shadyside space, making the new cafe a production kitchen as well as a neighborhood coffeehouse.

The story behind the transition is as warm as the coffee itself. George and Beth Saxon opened Georgie's Corner Cafe four years ago, building what regulars called an anchor of Walnut Street's walkable retail strip. When the Saxons decided to step away, they wanted to find a business that would honor what they had built rather than simply fill the square footage. They found that in TJ Fairchild and the Commonplace team.

"We're incredibly excited to step into this next chapter, building on the thoughtful foundation Beth and George created over the past few years."

TJ Fairchild, Founder, Commonplace Coffee

For Fairchild, the opening reflects a longer arc of investment in Pittsburgh neighborhoods. Commonplace launched with a single location and has expanded steadily across the city and its inner ring, always with the same philosophy: source well, roast carefully, and build spaces people actually want to spend time in. The Shadyside cafe will add that same ethos to Walnut Street's commercial corridor, a stretch that draws foot traffic from Shadyside, Squirrel Hill, and Oakland year-round.

A Kitchen Comes to Pittsburgh

The more significant news may be what happens behind the counter. Commonplace's commercial bakery has operated out of Indiana, Pennsylvania since 2015, producing the scratch-made pastries and baked goods that have become a signature of the brand. The Shadyside location will bring a meaningful portion of that production to Pittsburgh for the first time, putting the bakers closer to their customers and opening new possibilities for the menu.

Natalie Prunty, Commonplace's Bakery Manager, described the move as a genuine expansion of what the bakery team can do. "Having a kitchen location in Pittsburgh is so exciting because it gives our team the opportunity to grow and try new things in a manner that is more specific to our Pittsburgh guests," Prunty said. "We've never operated a bakery where our team gets to experience the guest's immediate interactions with our products, and I am so excited to see how that level of feedback and relationship can help us to grow and expand even more."

That feedback loop is exactly what distinguishes a neighborhood coffeehouse from a production facility. When bakers can watch someone take their first bite of a cream scone or a fresh pastry, the entire creative process changes. Commonplace is betting that proximity to its Pittsburgh customer base will accelerate the bakery's evolution in ways that distance from Indiana could not allow.

A Graceful Handoff on Walnut Street

George Saxon, reflecting on the transition, was candid about the emotional weight of closing a business he and Beth spent years building. "Our biggest hope was that we could find a local business to take over the Georgie's cafe space who would continue some elements of what we'd been doing," he said. "We are so happy that Commonplace Coffee is it." Saxon cited the shared values around ethically sourced coffee and the high standard of pastry that defined Georgie's as the reasons Commonplace felt like the right fit. "Georgie's provided an upbeat community space, which makes life better for so many of us, and Commonplace will continue to provide this."

Walnut Street does not have a shortage of places to spend an afternoon, but it has historically lacked a specialty coffee anchor with the depth of roasting identity and food program that Commonplace brings. The new location fills that gap, and does so with the credibility of a brand that Pittsburgh coffee drinkers have trusted for well over a decade. The Indiana bakery, which is not disappearing entirely, will continue to operate in a modified capacity, maintaining supply for Commonplace's other locations while the Shadyside kitchen grows into its own.

For a city that is increasingly attracting regional and national food brands, Commonplace's expansion tells a different kind of story: a Pittsburgh business, built here, growing here, and deepening its commitment to the neighborhoods where its customers live. When the doors open on Walnut Street this summer, the line for a cortado and a scratch-made morning bun will likely form quickly. Shadyside has been waiting for exactly this.
