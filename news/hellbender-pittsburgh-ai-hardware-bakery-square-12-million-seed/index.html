Hellbender Raises $12.5M to Scale Pittsburgh's First AI Camera Maker Into Bakery Square

The hardware startup, founded in 2021, closed a seed round led by local firm Magarac Venture Partners and is expanding to 40,000 square feet on Pittsburgh's "AI Avenue" â joining Google, Duolingo, and CMU Cloud Lab in the city's fastest-growing tech corridor.

By Rachel Thornton May 29, 2026 4 min read

Bakery Square in Pittsburgh's East End, where Hellbender is expanding its AI hardware manufacturing operations. // Photo: Staticshakedown / CC BY-SA 4.0 / Wikimedia Commons

Pittsburgh has long made its name manufacturing things the world needs. Now, one of the city's fastest-growing startups is betting that the next great thing to build here is the hardware that lets machines see.

Hellbender Inc., Pittsburgh's first domestically focused AI hardware manufacturer, closed a $12.5 million seed round on May 19 â its first traditional venture raise â and announced plans to expand its operations into Bakery Square's Bakery Office Four, a 40,000-square-foot facility that will become the company's new headquarters. The raise was co-led by Pittsburgh-based Magarac Venture Partners and Delaware's Veredas Partners, with additional participation from Mana Ventures, Gaingels, Sum VC, and local group Active Angels Network.

The company makes on-edge AI camera systems: smart vision devices that process information directly on the hardware itself, without routing data to the cloud. That distinction matters enormously for industries where latency, privacy, and cost are all on the line. Current customers are deploying Hellbender cameras in wound care settings at hospitals, autonomous navigation systems, and electrical infrastructure safety monitoring â anywhere, as chief growth officer David Tusick put it, that "needs to engage in the physical world."

"It's absolutely essential that we're in Pittsburgh. There is such a strong community here and it's like nowhere else."

David Tusick, Chief Growth Officer, Hellbender Inc.

Hellbender's product line spans three camera platforms. The Stereo Camera is built for robots and machines that need depth perception. The Vine Camera System connects multiple cameras across large facilities under a single monitoring hub. The Tadpole Camera is a compact, embeddable unit suited for custom hardware builds, insurtech platforms, and security applications. All three are manufactured in the United States, currently at the company's Harmar facility, which houses a printed circuit board assembly line inside an ISO Class 7 clean room.

Building on AI Avenue

The move to Bakery Square is more than a real estate decision. Hellbender's new home sits at the center of what the region's tech community has come to call "AI Avenue" â a stretch of the East End that now hosts more than two dozen AI companies, anchored by Google's Pittsburgh engineering office, Duolingo's global headquarters, UPMC Enterprises, and the CMU Cloud Lab. For a hardware company whose cameras are purpose-built to serve AI systems, the proximity to software and research partners is a meaningful advantage.

Jay Katarincic, a partner at Magarac Venture Partners, said the decision to lead the round came down to a simple observation about where the market is heading. "As AI and robotics move from research to real-world deployment, the need for secure, onshore engineering and production has never been more important," Katarincic said in announcing the investment. "Hellbender is uniquely positioned at the center of that shift. Their ability to nearly double revenue each year since founding reflects both strong execution and accelerating demand."

Founder Brian Beyer started the company in 2021 at a moment when most AI investment was flowing to software. His thesis was simpler: the software needs eyes. The circuit boards Hellbender assembles hold the chips that let AI run at the edge â on the camera itself, without a cloud dependency. That lowers costs for customers and opens the technology to industries where cloud connectivity isn't viable or isn't permitted.

Jobs, Wages, and the Manufacturing Model

Beyer has been deliberate about the kind of company Hellbender is building inside Pittsburgh, not just the product it is making. Manufacturing jobs at the Harmar facility start at $25 per hour, a rate Beyer credits to a hybrid model that pairs human assemblers with collaborative robots on the line. The robots handle repetitive tasks like fastening and quality control; the people handle judgment and complexity. That division keeps labor costs manageable enough to pay above-market wages.

The Bakery Square expansion will add a second manufacturing site and room for the engineering and software teams the company intends to hire aggressively over the coming months. Tusick said the company plans to recruit directly from Carnegie Mellon University, the University of Pittsburgh, and other regional schools â keeping Pittsburgh-trained talent in Pittsburgh. "Because our core focus is domestic hardware manufacturing and physical AI," he said, "nearly all of the work we do requires our team members to be physically present at our Pittsburgh facility."

That local-first commitment matters in a funding environment that has increasingly pushed Pittsburgh's most promising startups toward coastal capital and, sometimes, coastal relocation. Hellbender's round did include outside investors â Veredas Partners is Delaware-based, and several others are national â but the co-lead came from a Pittsburgh firm, and the company's manufacturing footprint remains firmly rooted in Allegheny County.

For a city that built its identity on making durable things, the story of a startup manufacturing AI hardware downtown is a satisfying one. Hellbender is not just building cameras. It is building the argument that Pittsburgh can be where physical AI is assembled, shipped, and scaled â not just where the ideas begin.
