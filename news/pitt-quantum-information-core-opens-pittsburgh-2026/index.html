Pitt Opens $11.6 Million Quantum Lab to Power Pittsburgh's Next Tech Frontier

The University of Pittsburgh's new Western Pennsylvania Quantum Information Core gives regional researchers and industry partners access to some of the rarest quantum instrumentation in the country, anchoring Pittsburgh's ambitions in the science that will define the next generation of technology.

The Pittsburgh Wire Staff May 26, 2026 4 min read
WPQIC
The Western Pennsylvania Quantum Information Core opened at Pitt's Old Engineering Hall in Oakland on May 4, 2026. Photo: University of Pittsburgh / Aimee Obidzinski

On the morning of May 4, a ribbon was cut in a laboratory on the University of Pittsburgh's Oakland campus that may one day be remembered as a turning point for Pittsburgh's technological identity. The Western Pennsylvania Quantum Information Core — WPQIC for short — opened its doors to researchers, industry leaders, and civic officials, marking the arrival of a $11.6 million facility that places Pittsburgh among a very small number of American cities capable of conducting frontier quantum science.

The facility is housed in Pitt's Old Engineering Hall and backed by the inaugural investment from Pitt's Strategic Advancement Fund. It features dilution refrigerators capable of cooling materials to 20 millikelvin, a temperature just a hair above absolute zero and far colder than anything found in outer space. At those temperatures, atomic particles begin behaving in ways that defy everyday intuition: existing in two states simultaneously, tunneling through seemingly solid barriers. These are the properties that make quantum computing, quantum sensing, and quantum communication possible. WPQIC also houses cryogenic magnetic equipment and high-powered microscopes that allow researchers to synthesize, fabricate, and characterize materials under extreme conditions, all within a single integrated research ecosystem.

WPQIC By the Numbers
$11.6M Investment from Pitt's Strategic Advancement Fund to build and equip the facility
20 mK Temperature (millikelvin) reached by the lab's dilution refrigerators, colder than deep space
13 yrs Years since Pitt co-founded the Pittsburgh Quantum Initiative with CMU and Duquesne University

Directed by Sergey Frolov, a professor in the Dietrich School's Department of Physics and Astronomy and a nationally recognized researcher in quantum materials and devices, WPQIC is structured as a shared resource open not only to Pitt faculty and graduate students but to regional academic partners and industry collaborators. That open-access model is deliberate. Quantum instrumentation of this caliber is extraordinarily expensive and difficult to maintain; by pooling access, Pitt is making Pittsburgh a destination for researchers and companies that could not otherwise afford to work at this level.

"Quantum science is at the heart of technology our society relies on every minute. This investment will help push the entire region to become a center for discovery and innovation."
Rob A. Rutenbar, Senior Vice Chancellor for Research, University of Pittsburgh

The ribbon-cutting drew an audience that reflected how seriously Pittsburgh's civic and academic leadership is taking quantum science. Pitt Chancellor Joan Gabel joined Rutenbar at the ceremony, as did Pittsburgh Mayor Corey O'Connor, who framed the investment in explicitly economic terms. "Pittsburgh has been able to thrive in large part because of its ability to develop and embrace cutting-edge technology," O'Connor said. "Institutions in this region have built a rare concentration of talent. Our job in government is to cultivate and capitalize on that talent." Sean Plasynski, acting director of the National Energy Technology Laboratory, was among the industry leaders who toured the facility that morning, signaling federal interest in what Pittsburgh is building.

A Decade in the Making

WPQIC did not emerge overnight. Its roots trace back to 2013, when Pitt co-founded the Pittsburgh Quantum Initiative alongside Carnegie Mellon University and Duquesne University. That collaboration laid the scientific groundwork and built the regional relationships that made a facility of this scale possible. Pitt has since added one of the nation's first undergraduate degree programs in quantum computing, ensuring a pipeline of trained talent to actually use the new lab. The Keystone AI + Quantum Factory, a statewide innovation initiative involving seven Pennsylvania research universities announced earlier this spring, positions WPQIC as a cornerstone of something even larger.

For Pittsburgh, the significance is straightforward. The city has spent the better part of two decades cultivating a reputation as a hub for robotics, artificial intelligence, and life sciences. Quantum science is the natural next frontier, and WPQIC gives Pittsburgh a credible claim to leadership in it. Industries from healthcare to finance to defense are watching quantum research closely, seeking the moment when laboratory breakthroughs translate into commercial applications. By establishing this infrastructure now, Pittsburgh is positioning itself to be the place where some of those breakthroughs happen, and where companies will want to be when they do.
